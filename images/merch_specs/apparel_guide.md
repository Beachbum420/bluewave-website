# Blue Wave Construction — Apparel & Branding Guide

A complete buying guide for Vuori (with your 60% off discount) plus what to put the logo on, where, and using which decoration method.

---

## THE STRATEGIC FRAMEWORK

You're going to be in five distinct scenarios with five distinct dress codes. Buying the right piece for each — and branding it the right way — makes you look intentional rather than improvised.

| Scenario | Goal | Decoration |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Investor/banker meetings | "I'm a developer, take me seriously" | None or subtle |
| 2. Realtor/agent showings, broker meetings | "Sharp local pro" | Subtle, premium |
| 3. Casual client meetings, walkthroughs | "Established but approachable" | Logo present, restrained |
| 4. Active jobsite work | "Working" | Bold, durable |
| 5. Employees / crew uniforms | "Crew, identifiable" | Bold, simple, cheap to reproduce |

Different garments serve different scenarios. Don't put a giant chest logo on the shirt you'll wear to a $5M deal pitch. Don't waste an unbranded premium polo on a demo day.

### TIER 3: JOBSITE / ACTIVE WORK (Bold branding, durable)

Now you can be loud. You're identifiable from across a job site, on a jobsite photo on Instagram, on a video walkthrough.

**Vuori is NOT the right brand for actual labor.** The performance fabrics will pill, snag, and stain on a real construction site. Use Vuori for "I'm going to walk a site and meet the framers for 30 minutes" — not "I'm swinging a hammer all day."

For actual jobsite work, buy from **Carhartt, Dickies, or Truewerk**:

**Carhartt K87 Pocket Tee** (the workwear standard)
- *Buy:* 4–6 in navy, black, heather grey
- *When:* Real labor days, demolition, framing, anything dirty
- *Branding:* **Screen print, large back, smaller left chest.** Bone-colored ink on dark shirts. This is your billboard — when you're carrying lumber and someone drives by, they should be able to read "BLUE WAVE CONSTRUCTION" from 30 feet.

**Carhartt Loose Fit Heavyweight Long Sleeve**
- *Buy:* 2 navy
- *When:* Cooler-weather labor
- *Branding:* Same — screen print, left chest + back

**Carhartt Rugged Flex Twill Shirt (Button Up Workshirt)**
- *Buy:* 2 navy or charcoal
- *When:* "Cleaner" jobsite — meeting with subs, inspector visits, when you need pockets and a collar but not full polish
- *Branding:* **Embroidered left chest only.** Workwear with embroidered logos = trade pro. Workwear with big screen prints = laborer. You want the former.

### TIER 5: HATS

The single highest-ROI branded item. People wear hats forever. Spend money here.

**Recommended hats — buy from Richardson directly or via a hat shop, not Vuori (Vuori hats are limited):**

1. **Richardson 112 Trucker** (the classic)
   - *Color:* Navy crown / bone front panel, OR all bone, OR all navy
   - *Branding:* **Embroidered front center** — use compact stacked logo (`05_logo_compact_navy.svg`)
   - *Use:* Daily jobsite, casual meetings, giveaways

2. **Richardson 110 Premium** or **Imperial 5054 Performance Cap** (low-profile, no foam)
   - *Color:* Navy or bone
   - *Branding:* **Embroidered front center**, smaller and tighter than the trucker
   - *Use:* The "premium hat" — wear when you want hat-on-head but not full trucker energy

3. **Richardson 254 Premium Wool Blend Beanie** or knit beanie
   - *Color:* Navy or charcoal
   - *Branding:* **Small embroidered patch or direct embroidery on cuff** (just the wave signature mark or "BW" — no full wordmark on a beanie, it'll be illegible)
   - *Use:* Cold mornings, photo content, employee gifts

**Hat embroidery rules:**
- Maximum logo width on a standard 6-panel hat front: **2.4 inches**
- Use **2 thread colors maximum** — for navy hats: bone wordmark + sky/wave underline. For bone hats: navy wordmark + wave-blue underline.
- Underlay matters — tell your embroiderer to use light underlay, not heavy. Heavy underlay puckers Vuori-grade fabric.

## DECORATION METHODS — WHAT GOES WHERE

| Method | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Embroidery** | Polos, premium tees, hoodies, hats, button-ups | Tactile, premium, ages well, looks expensive |
| **Screen print** | Workwear tees, jobsite hoodies, employee bulk orders | Cheap at scale, holds up to washing, allows large designs |
| **DTF (Direct to Film) transfers** | One-off promotional shirts, small batches | Quick turnaround, full color, but looks "printed" — keep off premium pieces |
| **Vinyl heat transfer (HTV) — your Cricut** | Personal/proto pieces, employee name tags, project-specific shirts | DIY, fast, fine for crew — DON'T put HTV on the pieces you wear to investor meetings |
| **Patches (sewn or velcro)** | Workwear button-ups, jackets | Looks legacy, easy to swap, great for crew jackets |

## WHERE TO GET BRANDING DONE (Los Angeles area)

- **Embroidery:** Local Embroidery + Screenprint shops in El Segundo, Hawthorne, or Gardena. Search "contract embroidery South Bay." Get 3 quotes. Average should be $7–12 per garment for left-chest embroidery in quantities of 12+.
- **Screen printing:** Same shops typically do both. Average $4–8 per shirt in quantities of 24+.
- **DIY (your Cricut):** Your Cricut handles vinyl heat transfer well — use it for one-off employee shirts, project-specific shirts, prototypes. Use the `03_logo_horizontal_black.svg` file for cutting (or invert in Cricut software for white vinyl on dark shirts).

**Do NOT use your Cricut for the shirts you wear to professional meetings.** HTV vinyl has a distinct look that reads "DIY." Save Cricut for crew shirts and prototypes.

## EMPLOYEE / CREW UNIFORMS

When you start hiring crew, you want them identifiable, branded consistently, but you don't want to spend $80/shirt. Recommended setup:

- **Daily wear:** Carhartt K87 navy tee, screen-printed back ("BLUE WAVE CONSTRUCTION" + phone number), small left chest logo. Issue 5 per employee.
- **Cold weather:** Carhartt midweight hoodie, embroidered left chest, screen-printed text on back yoke. Issue 1 per employee.
- **Hat:** Richardson 112 navy/bone trucker, embroidered front. Issue 2 per employee.
- **Optional vest:** Hi-vis safety vest with logo printed on back. One per employee, kept in truck.

Total cost per employee outfitted: ~$180-250 in apparel.

## ONE LAST THING

When you wrap your truck, banner-up your project sites, and start hiring — you want to be able to point at any apparel piece, any sign, any document, any vehicle and say "yes, that's our brand." This guide is structured to make sure that happens. Don't let an over-eager print shop convince you to put a giant chest logo on a Vuori polo. Don't let a hat embroiderer add three thread colors when two will do. Don't let an employee buy their own t-shirt and "match the brand" with a different navy.

The discipline is the brand.
